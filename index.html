How to Calculate Your Freelance Rate

Setting the right freelance rate is one of the most important decisions you'll make as a freelancer. Charge too little and you'll burn out, struggle to cover expenses, and undermine your perceived value. Charge too much without context and you might lose clients. This calculator helps you find the data-driven minimum you need to charge — so you never work at a loss.

The formula is straightforward: take your desired annual income, add your yearly business expenses, then gross up the total for taxes. Divide that number by your total billable hours for the year (hours per week × working weeks). The result is the minimum hourly rate you must charge to meet your financial goals.

Why Most Freelancers Undercharge

Many freelancers make the mistake of calculating their rate like a salary. If you want to earn $75,000 per year and a full-time employee works ~2,080 hours, you might assume $36/hour is enough. But freelancers pay self-employment taxes (around 15.3% in the US), cover their own health insurance, pay for software and tools, and — crucially — don't bill every working hour. Admin tasks, marketing, proposals, and client communication eat 30–40% of a typical freelance workweek.

What to Include in Business Expenses

When calculating your rate, include all recurring business costs: software subscriptions (Adobe, Figma, Notion, etc.), internet, phone, co-working space, accounting software, professional development, equipment depreciation, marketing costs, and any professional liability insurance. These add up fast — most freelancers spend $500–$2,000/month on business expenses.

Understanding the Tax Gross-Up

Because you pay taxes on revenue before expenses, you can't simply add your tax rate to your target income. The calculator uses a "gross-up" formula: divide your net required income by (1 - tax rate). For example, if you need $81,000 (income + expenses) and have a 30% tax rate, your required revenue is $81,000 ÷ 0.70 = $115,714 — not $105,300 as a simple addition would suggest.

How to Set Your Daily and Project Rate

Your daily rate is simply your hourly rate multiplied by the number of billable hours in a typical workday (usually 6–8 hours). For project rates, estimate the total hours the project will take, multiply by your hourly rate, and add a 10–20% buffer for scope creep, revision rounds, and project management overhead. Always define scope clearly in your contract to avoid scope creep eroding your effective rate.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate my freelance hourly rate? ▼
To calculate your freelance hourly rate, add your desired annual income to your annual business expenses, then gross it up for taxes. Divide the total required revenue by your total billable hours per year (hours per week × working weeks per year). For example: if you need $75,000/year take-home, have $6,000 in expenses, and work 1,440 billable hours/year at a 30% tax rate, your minimum hourly rate is approximately $90/hour.
How many billable hours should a freelancer work per week? ▼
Most freelancers bill 25–35 hours per week. The remaining time goes to admin tasks, marketing, client communication, and business development. Starting freelancers often assume they can bill 40 hours, but 25–30 billable hours is more realistic for a sustainable full-time freelance business.
What tax rate should freelancers use? ▼
In the US, most freelancers should budget 25–35% for taxes. This includes self-employment tax (~15.3%) plus federal and state income taxes. Use 25% if you're in a low-tax state, 30% as a safe default, and 35% if you're in a high-tax state like California or New York. Always consult a tax professional for personalized advice.
What is a good freelance hourly rate? ▼
A good freelance rate depends on your skill, experience, and industry. Entry-level freelancers typically charge $30–$60/hour. Mid-level specialists charge $60–$120/hour. Experienced consultants charge $120–$250+/hour. The rates from this calculator are minimums — always add 20–50% above your calculated floor to account for value delivered, market rates, and negotiation room.
How do I calculate my freelance project rate? ▼
To calculate a project rate: estimate the total hours the project will take, multiply by your hourly rate, then add a 10–20% buffer for revisions and scope changes. For example, a 20-hour project at $100/hour = $2,000 base + 20% buffer = $2,400 total project price. Always define project scope in writing before starting work.
